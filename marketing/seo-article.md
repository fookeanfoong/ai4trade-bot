# Pre-Market Stock Signals: How to Build a Simple Daily Watchlist

Most retail traders lose money not because they can't find ideas, but because they have
*too many*, with no plan for **when to get in, where to get out, or how much to risk**. A
good pre-market routine fixes that. This is a plain-English guide to building one — and an
honest look at what a "signal" can and can't do.

> This article is educational. It is not investment advice, and nothing here promises
> profit. Trading involves the risk of losing money.

## What a pre-market signal actually is

A pre-market signal is just a **structured idea for the day**, decided *before the market
opens*, with four things attached:

1. **A ticker and a direction** — long (expecting up) or short (expecting down).
2. **An entry price** — where you'd get in.
3. **A stop-loss** — the price where you admit you're wrong and get out.
4. **A target** — where you'd take profit.

That's it. The value isn't magic prediction; it's **discipline**. Writing the plan down
before the bell stops you from chasing green candles and panic-selling red ones.

## Why "before the open" matters

Markets gap. News breaks overnight. A plan you made last night can be wrong by 9:30am. So
the single most important rule: **refresh your view on the latest pre-open information, and
don't trade a stale idea.** If the reason for a trade disappeared overnight (a deal, an
earnings miss, a macro shock), the signal is dead — skip it.

## How to build a short daily watchlist

You do **not** need 50 tickers. Three to five is plenty. A repeatable filter:

- **A fresh catalyst.** News from *today* or overnight, not a story the market priced in
  weeks ago.
- **Liquidity.** Stick to names that trade heavily, so you can get in and out cleanly.
- **Trend agreement.** Prefer buying strength that's *also* above its recent average, not a
  bounce inside a downtrend.
- **No chasing.** If a name already ran hard in your direction, the easy move may be gone.

## Setting entry, stop and target

Turn each idea into numbers:

- **Entry:** often just the market price at the open, or a specific level you'll wait for.
- **Stop-loss:** a fixed percentage below your entry for a long (e.g. −2% to −5%),
  inverted for a short. **Decide this before you enter, and honor it.**
- **Targets:** it helps to use two — sell half at a first target (e.g. +3%) and let the
  rest run to a second (e.g. +6%). This locks in some gain while leaving room.

The exact numbers matter less than **having them written down** and sticking to them.

## Position sizing: the part most people skip

Here's the math that saves accounts. Decide **how much you're willing to lose** on a trade,
not how much you hope to make. If you risk 1–2% of your capital per idea, a losing streak
is survivable. A single oversized position is how accounts blow up.

A simple approach: given your capital and the stop distance, size the position so that
hitting the stop costs a small, fixed fraction of your account. Spread across a few names
so one bad open doesn't take you out.

## What signals can't do

Be honest with yourself:

- **They don't guarantee profit.** Any tool that promises daily returns is lying or
  breaking the law.
- **They don't replace your judgment.** You place the trades; you own the outcome.
- **Track record matters.** Anyone can screenshot a winner. Ask to see the *full* record —
  losses included.

That last point is worth repeating: **a tool that hides its losses is hiding the truth.**
The useful ones show the whole log.
